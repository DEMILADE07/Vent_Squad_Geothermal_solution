We're Vent Squad. Our question was simple — can a Rotliegend geothermal scheme heat AND cool an Utrecht district? Our answer is yes, with one honest catch we'll reach in 90 seconds.

The problem, in one slide

  • District demand: 10 MWth heating (winter) + 5 MWth cooling (summer)
  • Reservoir target: Rotliegend sandstone (Lower Permian, ~2 km deep)
  • Four wells on the table: BLT-01, EVD-01, JUT-01, PKP-01
  • Why now: the Netherlands already runs ~20 producing Rotliegend doublets
Two halves: 60% subsurface, 40% surface. The precedent is real — we're not inventing a play, we're siting one.

Our recommendation — lead with the answer

  • Site a 2-doublet (4-well) scheme near BLT-01
  • Design A: geothermal + ATES + electric heat pumps

  • Net cut-off: Vsh ≤ 0.40 AND φ ≥ 0.08

Resource: probabilistic MWth — the verdict

  • Split-lognormal fit (hits all 3 points), tail bounded at a 300 m³/h pump ceiling
  • 1 doublet: P50 5.05 MWth, only 29% chance of 10 MWth
  • 2 independent doublets: P50 13.2 MWth, 62% → design basis
  • Reconciled to ThermoGIS ~1%; thermal breakthrough ~177 yr ≫ life

Independent doublets (not one draw doubled) and a physical pump cap — no 77 °C doublet beats the ~13 MWth Dutch benchmark. Honest, not massaged.

Our differentiator: a distribution, not a point. Independence + a physical pump cap are both correct and honest — 62% chance of clearing 10 MWth with headroom, while one doublet falls short.

Design A — geothermal + ATES + heat pumps

6 ATES pairs (not 4): per-pair capacity is uncertain (0.5–2 MWth), so we size off the low end — P(supply ≥ 5 MWth) = 99.8%.

ATES is a seasonal cold battery. We size off the peak AND the low-throughput end: 4 pairs would meet demand only 29% of the time, so we carry 6. Honest robustness.

As a distribution: heat LCOE P10/P50/P90 = 10.8 / 12.6 / 26.7 €/GJ — the upper tail is the cost of the resource under-delivering (→ stage the appraisal). Fundable: SDE++ ≈ 3.8 €/GJ (~€63/tCO₂, ~13 kt CO₂/yr) → equity IRR ≈ 21 %. ~2× the Dutch heat-only benchmark — and we explain exactly why.

The 2× gap is the punchline: a cooler, less productive reservoir needs 4 wells for 10 MWth where the benchmark needs 2 for 13. Cause and effect, Challenge 1 → Challenge 2.

Bonus — AI-assisted workflow

  • pipeline.py — one CLI, raw LAS → hybrid LCOE, reproducible end-to-end
  • LightGBM missing-log prediction, validated by leave-one-well-out
  • Cross-well R²: DTC 0.51 (usable) · RHOB 0.10 · NPHI −0.20
  • Fallback rule: R² < 0.50 → ThermoGIS deterministic. We prove it, not assume it.
The honesty is the point — log prediction doesn't generalise across these four wells, so we fall back. AI as a validated screen, not a number we quietly trust.

Does this travel? Applicability to African geothermal

  • The method transfers, the numbers don't. Probabilistic resource workflow, database reconciliation, financed LCOE engine and the AI pipeline are geology-agnostic.
  • Not a Rift template: East African geothermal (Kenya Olkaria/Menengai, Ethiopia Aluto/Corbetti) is high-enthalpy volcanic → power, a different model.
  • Where this fits: North African / intracratonic sedimentary basins (Algeria, Tunisia, Egypt — direct-use), and the hybrid-cooling half for cooling-dominated African cities.
We're honest a 77 °C Dutch sandstone doublet isn't a Rift template. The transferable asset is the workflow — and the cooling design may be more relevant on a hot, cooling-hungry continent.

Risks, assumptions, what we did not do

  • Thermal breakthrough quantified (Gringarten-Sauty): ~177 yr ≫ 30-yr life → constant-MWth validated. Other risks: ATES throughput (6 pairs → 99.8%), drilling-cost volatility; resource downside carried in the LCOE tail
  • Key assumptions: reinjection 35 °C (ΔT 42 °C), e-price €150/MWh, 6,000/2,000 load-hours
  • Out of scope (declared): heat-distribution network, permitting, 3-D/FEM thermal modelling
Naming what we didn't do builds trust. Each is a next-phase item, not a gap we missed.

The ask / next steps

  • Pilot first: one well → in-situ injectivity test → confirm Darcy → complete doublet → second doublet → FID
  • Close before FID: 3-D seismic, in-situ injectivity test, transient breakthrough sim

The resource is real but modest; two doublets are the honest minimum; Design A is the right scheme.
